### Backdoor Roth Conversion problem with a large Pre-tax IRA Account

The Pro-Rata Rule Is the Problem:  the backdoor Roth conversion works by making a non-deductible (after-tax) contribution to a traditional IRA and then converting it to Roth. The tax-free benefit only works cleanly if you have **no other pre-tax IRA money**.

With $900K+ in pre-tax IRA funds, the IRS requires you to treat **all your IRAs as one pool** when calculating the taxable portion of any conversion. This is the pro-rata rule. If you convert $6,500, only about **0.72%** of it would be tax-free ($6,500 / $906,500). The remaining ~$6,453 would be **fully taxable as ordinary income**.

### What I Should Do Instead

To fix the excess contribution, you have cleaner options:
- **Recharacterize it** — Move the $6,500 from Roth to a traditional IRA before the tax deadline (including extensions). If you're over the income limit for deductibility, it becomes a non-deductible traditional IRA contribution, which is fine.
- **Withdraw it as an excess contribution** — Remove the $6,500 plus any earnings attributable to it before the tax deadline to avoid the 6% excise tax. You'd owe income tax on earnings only.
- **Apply it to 2026** — If you haven't yet filed your 2025 return and haven't made a 2026 contribution, you may be able to re-designate it as a 2026 contribution.

**The 6% Excise Tax Reminder**:  If you do nothing and leave the excess in the Roth, the IRS charges a **6% excise tax per year** on the excess amount ($390/year) until it's corrected. So acting before your filing deadline (including extensions, so October 15, 2026) is important.

Form 8606
- TurboTax handles Form 8606 automatically — you don't need to fill it out manually.

#### How It Works in TurboTax

TurboTax generates Form 8606 behind the scenes based on your answers. You never directly "fill out" the form — you just answer the interview questions correctly.
#### For the recharacterization (2025 return)

- Go to Federal → Deductions & Credits → Retirement and Investments → Traditional and Roth IRA Contributions
- Enter the original $6,500 Roth contribution
- TurboTax will ask if you recharacterized it — say yes
- Enter the amount recharacterized ($6,500 plus any earnings)
- TurboTax automatically generates Form 8606 and notes the non-deductible traditional IRA contribution

You'll also need to enter the 1099-R that your IRA custodian will send you documenting the recharacterization. Make sure you get this from your broker (Fidelity, Schwab, Vanguard, etc.) — they issue it after you request the recharacterization.

#### Key Things to Get Right

Request the recharacterization from your broker first — TurboTax can't do that part. You have to contact them directly and ask to recharacterize your 2025 Roth contribution as a traditional IRA contribution.

Track the earnings — when you recharacterize, you move the $6,500 plus any gains or minus any losses since the contribution. Your broker calculates this.

Save your Form 8606 every year — when you convert in 2026, you'll need to reference your 2025 basis to avoid being double-taxed.

For the 2026 Conversion
- When you do the actual Roth conversion next year, TurboTax will again ask about IRA contributions and conversions. You'll enter your prior year basis (from your 2025 Form 8606) and it will correctly calculate that the $6,500 portion is after-tax and not taxable again.

The most important step right now is contacting your broker to initiate the recharacterization — that needs to happen before you file your 2025 taxes (or by October 15, 2026 with an extension).
